## Generating the 4097th token of a response. How much work?

You feed the model a 4096-token prompt. It produces logits, you sample one token. Now you feed back all 4097 tokens to get token 4098. Then all 4098 to get 4099. Inference is fundamentally a loop, and each iteration is at least as expensive as the last.

In the naive implementation, each iteration *also recomputes every layer's keys and values for the entire prefix*. Token 4098's generation does the work for tokens 1, 2, …, 4097 over again — even though those tokens haven't changed since the previous step. For an $n$-token response, the total work scales as $O(n^3 \cdot d)$: the cube comes from $n$ generation steps, each of which does $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ work over the prefix so far.

This is the largest, most obvious source of redundancy in transformer inference, and almost every modern serving stack fixes it the same way: by caching the keys and values per layer.

## The redundancy

At each generation step $t$, the model needs three projections of every token's embedding: $\mathbf{Q}$, $\mathbf{K}$, $\mathbf{V}$. Attention then uses **only the current token's query** ($\mathbf{q}_t$) against the **entire prefix's keys and values** ($\mathbf{K}_{1:t}$, $\mathbf{V}_{1:t}$).

But $\mathbf{K}_{1:t-1}$ and $\mathbf{V}_{1:t-1}$ are bit-identical to what they were on the previous step: the embeddings of tokens 1 through $t-1$ haven't changed, and neither have the projection weights. So keep them in memory.

$$
\mathbf{K}_{\text{cache}} \mathrel{{+}{=}} \mathbf{k}_t, \quad \mathbf{V}_{\text{cache}} \mathrel{{+}{=}} \mathbf{v}_t
$$

Per step, this turns work that grows linearly with $t$ (recompute all $t$ rows) into work that's constant in $t$ for the projections (compute one row) plus linear in $t$ for the attention dot products themselves (still need to attend to the whole cache).

<MathCode
  math="\mathbf{K}_{\text{cache}} \leftarrow [\mathbf{K}_{\text{cache}}; \mathbf{k}_t], \qquad \mathbf{V}_{\text{cache}} \leftarrow [\mathbf{V}_{\text{cache}}; \mathbf{v}_t]"
  code={`# Per layer, per generation step:
k_new = x_t @ W_k   # 1 × d  (one row, NOT the whole sequence)
v_new = x_t @ W_v   # 1 × d
q_new = x_t @ W_q   # 1 × d

# Append to the persistent cache.
k_cache = torch.cat([k_cache, k_new], dim=0)  # (t × d)
v_cache = torch.cat([v_cache, v_new], dim=0)

# Attend the new query against the full cache.
scores = q_new @ k_cache.T / math.sqrt(d)     # 1 × t
attn   = softmax(scores) @ v_cache            # 1 × d`}
  caption="One row in, one row out. Per-step projection cost is now constant in t; the only sequence-length term left is the attention itself, which is linear."
/>

<Callout title="Why the prompt is 'free'" targetInteractive="kv-cache-builder">
  Step 0 is the prefill: the prompt's K and V are computed once in both modes. The win comes during *generation*. This is why production serving usually reports two latencies separately — time-to-first-token (prefill) and time-per-output-token (decode). The cache helps only the second number, which is why long prompts feel slow even with a perfect cache.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**The cache turns generation from cubic to quadratic.** Naive total work is $O(n^3 \cdot d)$ — the bottleneck is rebuilding the projections every step. Cached total work is $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ — the attention dot product is the only sequence-length term left. For $n = 2048$ this is roughly a hundred-fold difference. Without the cache, ChatGPT-style streaming wouldn't be practical at all.

**Memory replaces compute.** The cache is roughly $2 \cdot n \cdot d \cdot L \cdot \text{bytes}$ in size — two matrices (K and V), per layer, per token, at fp16 or bf16. For a 7B model with $d = 4096$, $L = 32$ layers, at 2048 tokens, that's ${\sim}0.5$ GB. Per request. Multiply by simultaneous users and you see why serving infrastructure obsesses over cache eviction, paged attention, and quantized caches.

**Context length is a memory budget, not a math budget.** "How long can the model's context be?" reduces to "how much KV cache memory fits in GPU memory?" That's why every architecture shift in the last two years — grouped-query attention, multi-query attention, MLA, sparse attention — has been about reducing the per-token cache footprint, not about getting smarter attention. The standard fix the curriculum covers next is **grouped-query attention**: keep all the query heads, share K and V across groups, shrink the cache by exactly the group factor. It's what LLaMA-2 70B, Mistral, Qwen, and every modern production decoder uses.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Autoregressive generation is a loop, and naively each iteration redoes the work of all the previous iterations.** The KV cache fixes this by keeping prior keys and values in memory: $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ total instead of $O(n^3 \cdot d)$. **The cost paid is memory** — the cache itself can be larger than the model weights at long context lengths. **Context length is gated by cache memory**, not by the math, which is why so much modern architecture work targets the cache footprint specifically.

We've now seen the model run end-to-end at inference time: tokens in, embeddings, attention, sampling, repeat, with the cache making the loop fast per step.
