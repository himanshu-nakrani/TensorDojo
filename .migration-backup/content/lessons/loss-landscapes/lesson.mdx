## The cartoon and the reality

The previous lesson drew the loss as a U-shaped curve, dropped a marble on the side, and watched it roll to the bottom. The picture is correct in spirit and wrong in detail. A real neural network has millions to billions of parameters, so the "surface" is a billion-dimensional thing nobody can plot. What can be plotted are 2-D slices of it. Researchers have spent a decade studying those slices and the picture that emerged looks nothing like the textbook bowl.

This lesson is a tour of four canonical 2-D loss surfaces that show up over and over in real networks. Each one is a different way for gradient descent to disappoint you, and a different way to fix it.

## Surface 1: the bowl

The shape SGD was designed for. One global minimum, the function curves smoothly upward from there, the gradient at every point reliably points back toward the minimum. Pick a starting point, take small enough steps, you converge. This is the picture every intro stats course draws and the only picture in which the math is fully behaved.

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{bowl}}(x, y) = x^2 + y^2
$$

The gradient is $(2x, 2y)$. The step $-\eta \nabla \mathcal{L}$ shrinks both coordinates by a factor of $(1 - 2\eta)$ each iteration. As long as $0 < \eta < 1$, the marble walks straight to origin.

## Surface 2: the saddle

In a high-dimensional loss, *most critical points are saddles, not minima*. (The reasoning: at a critical point in $n$ dimensions, the Hessian is symmetric with $n$ real eigenvalues. For it to be a minimum, all $n$ have to be positive. The probability of that drops as $n$ grows; the probability of a mixed-sign eigenvalue set goes to 1.) The 2-D version is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{saddle}}(x, y) = x^2 - y^2
$$

At the origin, both partial derivatives are zero. But the second derivative in $x$ is positive (curving up) and in $y$ is negative (curving down). The origin is a minimum along one axis and a maximum along the other — a saddle. A naive optimizer that decides "gradient is small, must be done" gets stuck. SGD with momentum and any noise gets through fine, because the negative-curvature direction provides a force that pushes the iterate off the saddle.

This is the headline result of Dauphin et al. (2014): the problem with high-dimensional optimization is rarely local minima, it's saddles slowing convergence to a crawl.

<Callout title="Walking through a saddle" targetInteractive="loss-landscape">
  Pick "Saddle" and click along the x-axis (anywhere where y ≈ 0). SGD slides toward the origin along x — the gradient there is small but pointing inward. Then it slides *down* in y. The trajectory threads through the saddle and runs off the bottom of the plot. Now click somewhere with a large y component to begin with. The y-direction dominates immediately. Saddle traps depend on where you start.
</Callout>

## Surfaces 3 and 4: sharp vs flat minima

Two minima can have exactly the same loss value but very different *shapes*. A **sharp minimum** is a narrow, steep basin: small region of low loss, steep walls. A **flat minimum** is a wide, shallow basin: large region of low loss, gentle walls. Empirically, **flat minima generalize better than sharp ones** (Keskar et al., 2017). The intuitive argument: a flat minimum is robust — small perturbations to the parameters (which is what you get on new data) don't push you out of the low-loss region. A sharp minimum is fragile; you fell into the cup by training-set luck, and any small parameter change kicks you back out.

The same intuition explains why SGD with a non-trivial learning rate prefers flat minima. Each gradient step is noisy enough that it can climb out of a sharp basin but stays comfortably inside a wide one. Decreasing the learning rate too aggressively trades flat for sharp.

## What this changes about how you train

Three practical consequences fall out of this picture:

1. **Saddles are the bottleneck, not local minima.** This is why modern optimizers (Adam, momentum-SGD) all carry second-moment estimates — they accelerate through saddle points where vanilla SGD crawls.
2. **Learning-rate schedules exist for a reason.** A high LR early helps escape sharp basins and saddles; a low LR late helps settle into the flat one you found. Cosine and warmup-decay schedules are the standard answer.
3. **Batch size affects which minimum you reach.** Small batches add noise that helps SGD favor flat minima. Large-batch training tends to settle into sharper, less-generalizable solutions — the *generalization gap* of large-batch training. This is partly why the LR has to be scaled with batch size.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **The loss surface is not a bowl** — saddles, sharp basins, and flat basins all show up in real networks, and they behave very differently under the same SGD update. **High-dimensional optimization is dominated by saddles, not local minima**, which is why momentum-based and adaptive optimizers win. **Flat minima generalize better than sharp ones**, and the noise injected by small-batch SGD is part of what biases training toward them.

What you should walk away with: the next time you see a training curve plateau, your first guess shouldn't be "stuck in a local minimum" — it should be "stuck near a saddle." And when someone says "the LR is too high," you now know what curvature they mean.
