## Sinusoidal positional encoding worked. Why did the entire field switch to something else?

The previous lesson covered the Vaswani 2017 scheme: build a deterministic PE matrix, add it to each token's embedding once, never touch it again. It worked — the original transformer was trained on it, and the dot product of two PE vectors is a smooth function of position distance, which is the property attention needs.

But over the years three problems with the additive scheme accumulated. **One**, addition entangles content and position in the same vector. The model has to learn to disentangle them inside attention; in practice it does, but you're asking a single learned matrix to separate two qualitatively different signals. **Two**, extending to longer contexts than the model was trained on is awkward — the PE matrix is hard-coded to a max length; extrapolation past it requires tricks. **Three**, the inductive bias you actually want — "relative position matters, absolute position doesn't" — is *implicit* in sinusoidal-add (you have to do the math to see it falls out of the dot product) and *explicit* in the alternative this lesson covers.

The alternative is **rotary positional encoding** (RoPE). Every modern decoder-only LLM uses it: LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, GPT-NeoX, DeepSeek. The change is qualitative, not just quantitative.

## The trick

Don't add position to the embedding. Instead, *rotate* the Q and K vectors by an angle that depends on the position, *before* taking the dot product.

The rotation is applied per pair of dimensions: dimensions $(0, 1)$ get rotated together, $(2, 3)$ together, and so on. Pair $k$ at position $m$ rotates by angle $\theta_{m,k} = m \cdot \text{base}^{-2k/d}$, with $\text{base} = 10{,}000$ — exactly the same frequency schedule as sinusoidal PE.

The headline identity falls out of the basic property of 2D rotations: rotate by $A$, rotate by $-B$, total rotation is $A - B$. So the dot product of "Q rotated by position $m$" and "K rotated by position $n$" depends only on $m - n$ — not on $m$ and $n$ separately.

$$
R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\text{rope}(\mathbf{q}, m) \cdot \text{rope}(\mathbf{k}, n) = \mathbf{q}^\top R(\theta_{m-n})\, \mathbf{k}
$$

  code={`# Per attention head, per layer, per token:
def apply_rope(x, pos, base=10000):
    d = x.shape[-1]
    out = torch.empty_like(x)
    for k in range(d // 2):
        theta = pos * base ** (-2 * k / d)
        c, s = math.cos(theta), math.sin(theta)
        x0, x1 = x[..., 2*k], x[..., 2*k+1]
        out[..., 2*k]   = x0 * c - x1 * s
        out[..., 2*k+1] = x0 * s + x1 * c
    return out

# Then attend.
q_rot = apply_rope(q, pos_q)
k_rot = apply_rope(k, pos_k)
scores = q_rot @ k_rot.T / math.sqrt(d)`}

<Callout title="The relative-position property in aggregate" targetInteractive="rope-relativity">
  The curves are perfectly symmetric around zero — every pair contributes the same way for offset +5 as for offset −5, because what matters is |m − n|. (Sign matters for the rotation direction, but the averaged dot product is symmetric.) This is the property attention exploits: "the token 5 to my left" is treated similarly to "the token 5 to my right," which is what you want for most language modeling.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**Rotation, not addition.** Sinusoidal PE adds a fixed vector to the embedding; RoPE rotates Q and K inside attention. The embedding is left alone — content and position stay in separate operations. There's no PE matrix sitting in memory; the rotation is a few cosines and sines computed per token at runtime, free at inference.

**The dot product is a function of relative offset, by construction.** The defining identity $\text{rope}(\mathbf{q}, m) \cdot \text{rope}(\mathbf{k}, n) = \mathbf{q}^\top R(\theta_{m-n})\, \mathbf{k}$ means *no information about absolute position survives* the attention operation. The model can't tell the difference between "token at position 100 attending to token at position 95" and "token at position 5 attending to token at position 0." That's not a bug — it's the inductive bias that most language tasks actually want.

**Applied per layer, per head, per token — at runtime.** RoPE is not part of the embedding table; it's part of the attention computation. Every transformer block re-rotates Q and K before scoring. This makes context-length extrapolation tractable: scaling the frequencies (NTK-aware scaling, YaRN) lets a model trained on 4k context handle 32k or 100k with only mild fine-tuning. Sinusoidal PE doesn't extend nearly as cleanly.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **RoPE rotates Q and K by a position-dependent angle instead of adding a position vector to embeddings.** The rotation is applied per pair of dimensions, at geometrically-scaled frequencies, exactly like sinusoidal PE. **The dot product of two rotated vectors depends only on the relative offset $m - n$**, by the basic 2D rotation identity. **It's the modern default** — LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, GPT-NeoX, DeepSeek all use RoPE. And because it's a runtime operation rather than a baked-in PE matrix, it composes cleanly with the KV cache: rotate the new query, attend against the (rotated, cached) keys, done.

The model now has content (embeddings), position (RoPE), and a numerical attention mechanism that doesn't saturate at large $d_k$ or peek into the future.
