## Why divide by √(d_k)?

The previous lesson used $Q \cdot K$ as the attention score. That score is a sum of $d_k$ products of $N(0, 1)$ random variables. The variance of one such product is $1$, and the variance of the sum is $d_k$. As $d_k$ grows — from $16$ in early transformers to $128$ in modern heads — the raw score values spread wider. Wide scores push $\operatorname{softmax}$ to one entry; a one-hot softmax has zero gradient; the model stops learning.

The fix is a single number: $\sqrt{d_k}$. Divide every score by $\sqrt{d_k}$ and the variance returns to $1$ regardless of dimension. Pre-softmax scores look the same at any $d_k$. Softmax no longer saturates by default. This is "scaled dot-product attention", the single most important numerical trick in the original Transformer paper.

## The formula

For a $d_k$-dimensional embedding dimension:

$$
\operatorname{score}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{d_k}}
$$

## Reading the result

Two facts to internalize.

1. **Variance grows linearly with $d_k$.** $d_k = 1$ is a thin sliver; $d_k = 16$ is four times as wide; $d_k = 64$ is eight times as wide. Each is a Gaussian with mean zero and standard deviation $\sqrt{d_k}$. The text above the chart reads the empirical standard deviation of the $4{,}000$ samples and compares it to the theoretical $\sqrt{d_k}$.
2. **The scale toggle brings every $d_k$ back to a unit bell.** The toggle does not change the *shape* of the data — only the x-axis scale. The variance is $1$ at every dimension.

**Why $\sqrt{d_k}$ specifically?** If we divided by $d_k$, the scores would shrink too fast: the standard deviation would be $1/\sqrt{d_k} \to 0$ as $d_k$ grew, the softmax would approach a uniform distribution, and the model would not learn to attend. $\sqrt{d_k}$ is the unique divisor that normalises the variance to a constant, independent of $d_k$. The proof: $\operatorname{Var}(\sum_i q_i k_i) = \sum_i \operatorname{Var}(q_i)\operatorname{Var}(k_i) = d_k$ for independent unit Gaussians, and the standard deviation is $\sqrt{d_k}$.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **For unit-Gaussian $\mathbf{Q}$ and $\mathbf{K}$ of dimension $d_k$, the raw dot product has variance $d_k$.** The histogram gets wider as $d_k$ grows, even though the inputs themselves don't change. **Wide scores push softmax to saturate** — one entry absorbs essentially all the weight, the rest go to 0, gradients vanish. **Dividing every score by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the variance to 1 at every dimension.** The trick is purely a matter of *units*: it does not change what the model attends to, only the numerical scale of the scores. The attention output is now numerically well-behaved at any $d_k$. The next lesson adds the final structural rule for autoregressive generation: a position is not allowed to attend to its future, so we mask the upper triangle of the score matrix — **causal masking**.
