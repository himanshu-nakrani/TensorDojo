## Why can a transformer be 12 layers deep without everything collapsing?

The previous lessons gave us attention in every form a production block uses — multi-head, grouped-query, flash-tiled, sliding-window. What the block doesn't have yet is the structural plumbing that lets you stack a dozen of these on top of each other. A 12-layer transformer is a stack of 12 sublayers, each of which is a non-linear function $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \operatorname{sublayer}(\mathbf{x})$. Naively, the gradient of the loss with respect to the input is the product of 12 Jacobians. The spectral norm of each Jacobian is rarely exactly 1 — for transformer-style sublayers it is typically slightly less than 1, so after 12 multiplications the gradient *vanishes* and the early layers stop learning. (The opposite case, exploding, can also happen if the sublayer is poorly initialized; both are failures of the same product-of-Jacobians problem.) Either way, depth is the enemy.

The fix is two ideas that always travel together in the architecture:

- **Residual connections**: $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \operatorname{sublayer}(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{x}$ instead of $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \operatorname{sublayer}(\mathbf{x})$. The gradient of the loss with respect to the input has a direct path through the "+1" identity, so it never has to flow through the sublayer's Jacobian.
- **Layer normalization**: $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \gamma \cdot \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta$ for each token's vector independently, where $\mu, \sigma^2$ are the per-token mean and variance. This keeps activations on a stable scale across depth, so the residual stream does not drift toward zero or infinity.

The two together: residuals let gradients flow, layernorm keeps the activations the gradients flow through on a sensible scale.

## The formula

$$
\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \mathbf{x}_l + \operatorname{sublayer}_l(\mathbf{x}_l), \qquad
\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \operatorname{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{l+1}\right)
$$

In modern transformers, the layernorm is applied *before* the sublayer ("pre-norm"): $\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \mathbf{x}_l + \operatorname{sublayer}(\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf{x}_l))$. Both orderings are common in the literature; pre-norm is more numerically stable at depth and is what we will see.

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**The residual stream is the message that flows through the network.** Every sublayer reads from the residual stream and writes back into it. The model's final output for a token is the residual stream at the last layer, projected to vocab. The residual stream is *always* meaningful at every layer — the sublayers are *edits*, not *replacements*.

**Without residuals, the depth limit is brutal.** In a 12-layer model without residuals, the gradient through the network is the product of 12 Jacobians. Spectral norms slightly less than 1 (the typical case for transformer sublayers) drive the product to zero — gradients vanish into the early layers. Spectral norms slightly above 1 explode it. Residuals add a $+1$ to each Jacobian's diagonal, which the chain rule treats as "the gradient can also flow through unchanged". The result is a gradient that is at least 1 in magnitude (the residual path) plus the sublayer's contribution.

**Layernorm is the budget enforcer.** It re-centers each token's vector to zero mean and unit variance before every sublayer. This caps the magnitude of what the sublayer can read and write, so a single sublayer cannot blow up the residual stream. With layernorm, the model can stack 12, 24, 96 sublayers and still train.

The two together are not optional. Residuals give the gradient a path; layernorm keeps the path numerically stable. Drop either, and the model fails to train at depth.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **The residual stream is the message that flows through the network** — every sublayer reads from it and writes back, never replaces. The final output for a token is the residual stream at the last layer, projected to vocabulary. **Without residuals, depth is the enemy**: the gradient of the loss with respect to the input is the product of $N$ Jacobians, and transformer-style sublayers have spectral norm $\ne 1$, so the product collapses to 0 (or explodes). **Layernorm is the budget enforcer** — it re-centers each token's vector to zero mean and unit variance before every sublayer, capping what the sublayer can read and write. **Pre-norm is the modern convention**: layernorm before the sublayer, residual after. With these two, a 96-layer transformer trains. The next lesson trims the budget enforcer itself — modern LLMs replaced LayerNorm with the simpler **RMSNorm**, dropping the mean-subtraction and the learned shift, with no measurable quality loss.
