## If big weights mean the model is memorizing noise, what if we just... made big weights expensive?

The previous lesson ended on a cliffhanger: the gap between training and test loss grows when the model has too much capacity for the data. The reader's instinct is "make the model smaller" — drop the polynomial's degree, prune some weights. But the modern approach is different: keep the model the same, and *change the loss function* so that big weights are penalized.

The trick is one line.

## The formula

For a model with weights $\mathbf{w}$ and per-example loss $L(\mathbf{w}; x)$, the L2-regularized loss is

$$
L_{\text{reg}}(\mathbf{w}; x) = L(\mathbf{w}; x) + \lambda \|\mathbf{w}\|^2
$$

where $\lambda \ge 0$ is the **weight-decay coefficient**. The gradient of the regularized loss is

$$
\nabla L_{\text{reg}} = \nabla L + 2\lambda \mathbf{w}
$$

so the SGD update becomes

$$
\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta (\nabla L + 2\lambda \mathbf{w}) = (1 - 2\eta\lambda) \mathbf{w} - \eta \nabla L
$$

The $2\lambda \mathbf{w}$ term shrinks every weight by a fixed factor $(1 - 2\eta\lambda)$ per step, regardless of what the data says. *Big weights are pulled toward zero unless the data evidence pushes them away.* (Library implementations absorb the factor of 2 into $\lambda$ — that's why PyTorch and the AdamW form below show $\eta \lambda \mathbf{w}$, not $2\eta\lambda \mathbf{w}$. The two conventions differ by a factor of 2 in the numeric value of $\lambda$, but describe the same update.)

For a polynomial fit, the closed-form L2 version is the *regularized normal equations*

$$
(X^\top X + \lambda I)\,\mathbf{w} = X^\top \mathbf{y}
$$

(with the convention that the bias term is *not* regularized). The ridge $\lambda I$ keeps the system well-conditioned for high-degree polynomials, which is the case where the unregularized $X^\top X$ is rank-deficient.

## AdamW: the modern decoupled form

The naive form above adds $\lambda \|\mathbf{w}\|^2$ to the loss. With Adam (which adapts the per-parameter step size by dividing by an estimate of the gradient magnitude), this couples the decay to the Adam denominator — large historical gradients *shrink* the effective decay. Loshchilov & Hutter (2019) showed that a *decoupled* form is simpler and works better — the decay is a separate, plain $\eta \lambda \mathbf{w}$ subtraction that bypasses the Adam preconditioner:

$$
\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} \;-\; \eta \cdot \frac{\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t} + \varepsilon} \;-\; \eta \, \lambda \, \mathbf{w}
$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t, \hat{\mathbf{v}}_t$ are the bias-corrected Adam moments. The first term is the standard Adam update; the second is the decoupled weight decay.

Every modern transformer (BERT, GPT, LLaMA) trains with AdamW. The bias term is conventionally excluded — biases are not "capacity" the way weights are. The two forms (L2-in-loss vs decoupled) are *different* updates under Adam: the L2 form couples decay to the historical-gradient scale; the AdamW form keeps decay at a clean $\eta \lambda \mathbf{w}$ regardless of what Adam's preconditioner is doing.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **L2 weight decay adds $\lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$ to the loss**, equivalent to a per-step shrinkage of every weight by a fixed factor. **It trades fit for simplicity** — λ=0 is the unregularized baseline; as λ grows, test loss bottoms at a sweet spot before climbing again as the model under-fits. **AdamW uses the decoupled form** ($w \leftarrow w - \eta(g + \lambda w)$) — every modern transformer trains this way, and the bias is excluded by convention. The next lesson: a different kind of regularization that doesn't touch the loss at all, but instead touches the *network* at every training step. That is dropout.
