## Which key is most relevant to this query?

A model has a query vector $Q_i$ and a row of key vectors $K_0, K_1, \ldots, K_{n-1}$. For each pair, it needs a number: "how well does this key answer this query?". That number is the **attention score** — a raw dot product, $Q_i \cdot K_j$.

The score alone is not enough. The model's softmax in the next step will turn the scores into a probability distribution over the keys, allocating the query's attention. The transformed scores are the **attention weights** — non-negative, summing to one across each row.

The previous lesson was the building block (dot product). This one is the next layer: the **score matrix** $Q K^\top$ is the input, the **weight matrix** is the row-wise softmax of that. The two are not the same thing; confusing them is the most common slip in attention code.

## The formula

For a row of $n$ query vectors $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ and a row of $n$ key vectors $K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$:

$$
\text{scores} = Q K^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}, \qquad
\text{weights}_i = \operatorname{softmax}(\text{scores}_i) \text{ per row}
$$

The score matrix is one dot product per (query, key) pair. The weight matrix is the row-wise softmax.

## Reading the result

Read the matrices column by column. The score matrix's column $j$ is the dot product of every query with $K_j$. The weight matrix's column $j$ is how much of each query's attention is allocated to $K_j$ — and each row sums to one.

Two facts to internalize.

1. **The score matrix can have any sign and any magnitude.** The dot product has no built-in scale.
2. **The weight matrix is always non-negative and each row sums to one.** Softmax enforces that, no matter what the input scores look like.

This is why a model can use raw attention scores as a "how aligned" signal in attention probing, but to actually *route* information, it needs the weights.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **For a row of queries and a row of keys, the score matrix is one dot product per (query, key) pair** — $\text{scores} = Q K^\top$, an $n \times n$ matrix. **The weight matrix is the row-wise softmax of the score matrix.** The two are not the same thing; confusing them is the most common slip in attention code. **Scores can be any real number; weights are non-negative and sum to one across each row.** This is why the model can probe with raw scores but must route information with weights. The next lesson uses the weights: multiplying $W$ by the value vectors $V$ gives a per-token mix — the attention output, which is what each token actually receives.
