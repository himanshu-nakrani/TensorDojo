## Why "let's think step by step" actually works

The in-context learning lesson showed how examples in the prompt change a frozen model's behavior. **Chain-of-thought** prompting is a particular kind of in-context example — instead of demonstrating `Q → A`, you demonstrate `Q → reasoning steps → A`. The 2022 paper that introduced the term measured a striking effect: the same models that scored 17% on a multi-step math benchmark with direct prompting scored 57% when prompted to think step by step. No fine-tuning. No new weights. Just a different prompt template.

The mechanism is mechanical, not magical. A transformer's forward pass has a **fixed amount of compute per token**: $L$ layers, each doing one attention + one MLP step. For a hard problem that needs more compute than that, asking for the answer in one token forces the model to do all the reasoning inside that single forward pass — which it usually can't. Asking for *intermediate tokens* lets the model spread the computation across many forward passes: each step's tokens become part of the context for the next step. The reasoning is offloaded from the model's hidden activations into the visible token stream.

This is why chain-of-thought disproportionately helps on **multi-step tasks** (arithmetic, logic puzzles, planning) and barely helps on **single-step tasks** (lookups, simple classification). The trick only buys you something when the problem actually needs more compute than one forward pass can deliver.

## The formula

For a problem with answer $a$, given a question $q$, direct decoding samples:

$$
a \sim p_\theta(a \mid q)
$$

Chain-of-thought decoding samples a reasoning trace $r = (r_1, \ldots, r_T)$ *and then* the answer:

$$
r \sim p_\theta(r \mid q), \qquad a \sim p_\theta(a \mid q, r)
$$

The second decode is conditioned on the trace. Each intermediate $r_t$ is sampled one at a time, with each new token added to the context — so the model gets to attend to its own previous reasoning before predicting the next step.

The marginal probability of the right answer is:

$$
p_\theta(a^* \mid q) = \sum_r p_\theta(a^* \mid q, r) \cdot p_\theta(r \mid q)
$$

For problems where some $r^*$ exists such that $p_\theta(a^* \mid q, r^*) \gg p_\theta(a^* \mid q)$ — i.e., the right reasoning trace makes the answer easy — sampling the trace first dramatically increases the chance of the right answer. For problems where no useful trace exists, CoT doesn't help.

## Reading the result

**The mechanism is amortization.** A hard problem takes more compute than the model has per token. CoT lets the model use *multiple* forward passes — one per intermediate token — by writing partial results into the context. Each forward pass is still bounded by the model's depth, but the *total* compute the model brings to bear is now `depth × tokens`, not just `depth`.

**CoT helps multi-step tasks, not lookups.** Asking a model "What's the capital of France?" with CoT doesn't help — the answer is one lookup, the model's forward pass already handles it. Asking the same model "If a train leaves Boston at 3pm at 60mph and another leaves NYC at 4pm at 80mph, when do they meet?" — that's where CoT shines. The improvement scales with the number of reasoning steps the problem actually requires.

**CoT emerges at scale.** Small models (under ~10B parameters) often don't benefit from CoT — they can't reliably generate coherent intermediate steps, so the trace adds noise rather than signal. Above ~62B parameters the effect becomes large; the 2022 paper called this an "emergent" capability. Modern reasoning-tuned models (o1, R1, Gemini 2.5 Thinking) are *trained* to produce long traces and benefit from CoT in proportion to how long they're allowed to think.

**Variants and extensions.** Zero-shot CoT ("let's think step by step") — surprisingly effective on capable models. Self-consistency — sample many traces, take the majority answer; reliably better than single CoT. Tree-of-thoughts — branch and prune over traces; useful for problems with verifiable subgoals. Reasoning models — RL-tune the model itself to produce long traces and to be rewarded for the final answer's correctness. All of them are variants of the same core idea: spread the computation across more tokens.

## A second look

CoT and ICL are siblings. Both work because the prompt is a software interface to a frozen network. ICL conditions the model on the *shape* of the task (Q/A pairs, classification, translation). CoT conditions it on the *process* of the task (step-by-step reasoning). The 2022 paper's "Let's think step by step" is a magic incantation only in the sense that the prompt is a programming language and "let's think step by step" is one of its most useful primitives.

<Callout title="The cost: tokens" targetInteractive="cot-explorer">
  CoT trades latency and cost for accuracy. A 500-token reasoning trace is 500× the inference cost of a single-token answer. For a customer support bot, that's expensive. For solving a hard math problem once, it's free. The economic question is always "how many extra tokens am I willing to pay for one more correct answer."
</Callout>

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Chain-of-thought is a prompting trick — no weights change.** The model emits intermediate reasoning tokens before committing to the answer; the answer is then conditioned on the trace. **The mechanism is amortization.** A hard problem needs more compute than one forward pass can provide; CoT spreads the computation across multiple forward passes, each conditioned on the last. **CoT helps multi-step tasks at scale.** Small models don't benefit; large models benefit dramatically; reasoning-tuned models benefit most.
