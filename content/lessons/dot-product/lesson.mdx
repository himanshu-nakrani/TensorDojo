## How aligned are two vectors?

A word embedding wants to know which other embeddings point the same way. A query wants to know which keys in a retrieval index are aligned with it. A classifier wants to know which side of a decision boundary a sample falls on. All of these are the same question, asked of two vectors. The answer is a single number.

**The dot product is that number.** It is the natural choice — symmetric, smooth, and built from a single sum of products — and every "how aligned?" computation in a neural network reduces to it. One formula; three ways to read it.

## The formula

For two equal-length vectors $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (b_1, \ldots, b_n)$:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \;=\; \|\mathbf{a}\| \, \|\mathbf{b}\| \, \cos\theta
$$

Three readings of one expression.

1. The **component form** $\sum a_i b_i$ is the implementation: multiply componentwise, sum. No square roots, no trig.
2. The **geometric form** $\|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$ is the meaning: $\cos\theta$ is the alignment, and the magnitudes scale it.
3. The **sign of the dot product** is the sign of $\cos\theta$: positive when acute, zero when orthogonal, negative when obtuse.

</Callout>

## Reading the result

Two traps to avoid.

**"The dot product measures the angle."** It measures the *cosine* of the angle, scaled by magnitudes. On unit vectors, dot product and cosine coincide. On arbitrary vectors, the magnitudes matter.

**"Bigger vector, bigger dot product."** Only if the direction is fixed. A long vector dotted with an orthogonal vector is still zero, no matter how long.

The three inputs (direction of $\mathbf{a}$, direction of $\mathbf{b}$, and the two magnitudes) are independent. The dot product reads them all at once, but the experiments above separate them. That separability is what makes the dot product useful: it factors naturally into an alignment part and a magnitude part, and you can study each in isolation by freezing the other.

A useful mental model: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$ is the answer to "if I were to measure how much of $\mathbf{a}$ lies in the direction of $\mathbf{b}$, what would the projection be?" The full projection comes next; the dot product is the unnormalized answer.

## Recap

Three takeaways to carry forward. **The dot product is a single number with two readings:** geometrically, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\| \, \|\mathbf{b}\| \cos\theta$ — the magnitudes scaled by alignment; computationally, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_i a_i b_i$. **Sign tells you which side of perpendicular the pair sits on; magnitudes tell you how strongly.** A length-2 vector dotted with an orthogonal one is still zero, no matter how long. **The three inputs (direction of $\mathbf{a}$, direction of $\mathbf{b}$, magnitudes) are independent**, which is why the dot product factors cleanly into direction and magnitude — and why cosine similarity, the magnitude-free form, is a separate operation.
