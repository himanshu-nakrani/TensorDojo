## You've built it, trained it, fine-tuned it. How do you know if it's any good?

The curriculum so far has been all about *making* models — attention, training loops, optimizers, fine-tuning. Not a single lesson has said how to know if the model you built actually works. That gap is conspicuous because *every* paper, every release, every leaderboard reduces to a single act of measurement: "this model scored X on benchmark Y."

Two kinds of metrics carry that weight. **Intrinsic** metrics measure how well the model models its training distribution — perplexity is the canonical example, and it's literally exp of the loss the model was trained on. **Extrinsic** metrics measure how well the model performs on downstream tasks the field cares about — MMLU on knowledge, HellaSwag on commonsense, HumanEval on code, GSM8K on math. Each kind has well-known failure modes; modern practice uses both and trusts neither completely.

Before LoRA closes the fine-tuning chapter and instruction tuning opens the alignment one, this lesson covers the measurement infrastructure both rely on. You cannot improve what you cannot evaluate.

## Perplexity, in one line

Perplexity is the exponential of the average per-token cross-entropy loss. If the model assigns log-probability $\ell_t$ to the true token at position $t$, then for $T$ tokens:

$$
\text{PPL} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t\right)
$$

A perfect model (probability 1 on every true token) has perplexity 1. A uniform model over a $V$-token vocabulary has perplexity $V$. Real LLMs on natural text land somewhere between 8 and 20 — meaning the model is, on average, as uncertain as a 10-way choice at every token.

The connection to training is exact: cross-entropy is the loss the model minimized. Perplexity is the same number in token-space units instead of nats. Lower training loss = lower perplexity, no surprises.

## Pass@k, accuracy, and the benchmark zoo

For downstream tasks the metric depends on the task shape:

- **Multiple-choice** (MMLU, HellaSwag, ARC, GPQA): accuracy. Pick the option with the highest log-probability, count hits over $N$ questions.
- **Code generation** (HumanEval, MBPP, SWE-bench): pass@$k$. Sample $n$ programs per task; the unbiased estimate of "probability that at least one of $k$ samples passes" is $1 - \binom{n-c}{k}/\binom{n}{k}$ where $c$ of the $n$ samples pass. Pass@1 (greedy) and pass@10 (with sampling) are the most-reported numbers.
- **Math** (GSM8K, MATH): exact-match on the final answer, sometimes with chain-of-thought required.
- **Open-ended generation** (MT-bench, AlpacaEval, Arena-Hard): pairwise preference judgments, often via another LLM as judge or human raters.

  math="\text{pass@}k = \mathbb{E}_{\text{tasks}}\!\left[\,1 - \binom{n-c}{k}/\binom{n}{k}\,\right]"
  code={`# HumanEval pass@k, the unbiased estimator from the original paper.
def pass_at_k(pass_results, k):
    """pass_results[i] is a list of booleans for task i's n samples."""
    out = []
    for row in pass_results:
        n, c = len(row), sum(row)
        if k > n:
            raise ValueError(f"k={k} exceeds samples per task n={n}")
        if n - c < k:
            out.append(1.0)
        else:
            ratio = 1.0
            for i in range(k):
                ratio *= (n - c - i) / (n - i)
            out.append(1 - ratio)
    return sum(out) / len(out)`}
  caption="The estimator avoids the bias of just counting 'any of the first k samples passes' — for small n this is the difference between an honest measurement and a noisy one."

<Callout title="Why perplexity isn't enough" targetInteractive="perplexity-calculator">
  Perplexity measures fit to the *training distribution*. A model that memorizes Wikipedia has low Wikipedia-perplexity but might be terrible at code. A model trained on Common Crawl with a heavy weighting on chat data has low chat-perplexity but might be useless for math. Perplexity is necessary (high perplexity = bad model) but not sufficient (low perplexity ≠ useful model). That's why downstream benchmarks took over as the headline metric.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**Perplexity is exact and limited.** It measures the same thing the model trained on, which makes it a clean signal during pretraining (loss going down = perplexity going down = model getting better at its training distribution). But it doesn't measure what we usually care about: can the model write code, solve math, answer questions. Two models with the same perplexity can have wildly different downstream utility.

**Benchmarks measure capability slices, not capability.** MMLU measures broad-knowledge multiple-choice; HumanEval measures Python function-completion; GSM8K measures grade-school math word problems. Each correlates only roughly with what users actually want. "Which model is best" is always a weighted question; the weights are usually implicit and rarely defended.

**Both metrics decay.** Perplexity loses resolution once you're below a certain loss (the model has internalized the distribution; further gains are in the long tail). Benchmarks saturate and contaminate. The field keeps inventing new ones (Arena-Hard, SWE-bench Verified, ARC-AGI, FrontierMath) specifically to keep measurement ahead of capability. Whatever metric you cite is probably out of date within 18 months.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Perplexity is exp of the cross-entropy the model trained on** — exact, intrinsic, useful during pretraining, weakly correlated with downstream utility. **Benchmarks measure narrow capability slices**, and the composite "best model" rank depends on which benchmarks you weight. **Both have known failure modes** — perplexity is shallow, benchmarks saturate and leak — and the field's response is a treadmill of new metrics.

That's the measurement infrastructure the rest of the curriculum implicitly relied on. The next and final lesson is the one this measurement infrastructure made possible at all: how the model learns from a *preference* signal instead of a label, the way the assistant flavor of every modern LLM is actually trained.
