## Attention mixes tokens. What rewrites the *content* of each token after that?

The previous lesson closed the residual-stream idea: each sublayer reads from the residual stream and writes back, never replaces. Attention is the cross-token sublayer — it produces a per-token output that mixes value vectors across the sequence. The *content* of each token, however, is mostly preserved by attention (a token attends heavily to itself, so the output is close to the input).

The piece that *rewrites* the content of each token is the **feed-forward network** — a two-layer MLP with a non-linearity, applied *independently* to each token's vector.

## The formula

For each token vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$, the FFN computes:

$$
\operatorname{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; W_2 \, \sigma(W_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2
$$

with $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{hidden}} \times d_{\text{model}}}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{hidden}}}$, and $\sigma$ a non-linearity. (The matrices left-multiply column vectors here. Frameworks that store $\mathbf{x}$ as a row vector and write $\mathbf{x} W_1$ flip both shapes — the parameter count is the same.) The convention is $d_{\text{hidden}} = 4 \cdot d_{\text{model}}$ (Vaswani et al., 2017) — the **expansion factor** is 4×. The FFN is applied position-wise: it sees one token at a time, no cross-token interaction.

<Callout title="Why expand, then project?" targetInteractive="feed-forward-explorer">
  The 4× expansion is the FFN's "thinking room." The first linear layer $W_1$ maps the token's vector from $d_{\text{model}}$ dimensions to a $d_{\text{hidden}}$-dimensional space where the model can express more features. The activation $\sigma$ is what makes this useful — without it, two linear layers in sequence collapse to one. The second linear layer $W_2$ projects back to $d_{\text{model}}$ so the output can write into the residual stream (which is $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional).

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**The FFN is position-wise.** Each token's vector is rewritten independently — the FFN has no notion of "the other tokens" and does not look at attention patterns. Attention mixes tokens; the FFN rewrites each one. The combination — attention output → residual add → FFN → residual add — is the transformer block.

**The expansion is where the parameters live.** A 4× expansion at $d_{\text{model}} = 4$, $d_{\text{hidden}} = 16$ gives $4 \times 16 = 64$ weight parameters in $W_1$ plus another $16 \times 4 = 64$ in $W_2$, plus 16 + 4 biases — 148 total in the FFN. The attention block (4 projections of $d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{model}}$ each, counting the output projection) lands at roughly $5 \cdot d_{\text{model}}^2 = 80$. Even at this toy size the FFN is already ~1.85× the attention; at real transformer widths ($d_{\text{model}}$ = 512, 1024, 4096) the gap widens further. Most of the parameters of a trained transformer are in the FFN, not the attention.

**The activation is the non-linearity that makes the FFN interesting.** Two linear layers in sequence, without a non-linearity, collapse to a single linear layer. GELU is the modern default; ReLU was used in the original Transformer paper. GELU is smooth at zero (letting small negative signals through), which empirically trains better for transformer FFNs.

## A second look

The "Where the parameters live" widget makes the FFN's role concrete. In a modern transformer with $d_{\text{model}} = 1024$ and 4× expansion, the FFN has $1024 \times 4096 \times 2 = 8.4$ million parameters per block; the attention block has $4 \times 1024 \times 1024 = 4.2$ million. Across 96 blocks, the FFN is ~$800$M parameters; the attention is ~$400$M. The routing is the attention; the thinking is the FFN.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **The FFN is a position-wise two-layer MLP** — $W_2 \sigma(W_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2$, applied to each token's vector independently. **The 4× expansion is where the parameters live** — most of a transformer's parameters are in the FFN, not the attention. **GELU (or ReLU) is the non-linearity that makes the FFN interesting** — without it, two linear layers collapse to one. This is the second sublayer in the block. Attention mixes tokens across the sequence; the FFN rewrites each token's content. Modern models exploit the FFN's parameter dominance with **mixture of experts** — replace one big FFN with N small ones, route each token to top-k — and that's the next lesson before we assemble the full block.
