## You've picked an optimizer. The single most important hyperparameter is the learning rate — and it shouldn't be constant.

The optimizer lesson picked the step equation. The SGD lesson sampled a batch. The data and the loss are fixed. The remaining knob is $\eta$ — the *learning rate* — and so far we've treated it as a single constant. In practice, the LR is the single most important hyperparameter in a training run, and it's almost never constant. It changes over the course of training in a schedule the engineer picks ahead of time.

Two reasons the LR should change: **early instability**, and **late oscillation**. Early in training, the model's parameters are at their random initialization, the loss surface is unfamiliar, and an adaptive optimizer (Adam) hasn't yet built up its per-parameter statistics. A large constant LR at this point blows the model up. **Warmup**: start with a small LR, ramp up to the peak over the first $W$ steps. Late in training, the model is near a minimum and the LR is still the same magnitude as in the early steps, so it overshoots the minimum and oscillates around it without settling. **Decay**: shrink the LR as training progresses so the model can settle. Modern training is almost always **warmup + cosine decay** — the two together are the conventional default.

## The formulas

For a step $t \in [0, T)$ with peak LR $\eta_{\max}$ and floor $\eta_{\min}$:

**Constant:** $\eta(t) = \eta_{\max}$.

**Linear decay:** $\eta(t) = \eta_{\max} \cdot \max(0, 1 - t/T)$.

**Cosine decay:** $\eta(t) = \eta_{\min} + (\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min}) \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(1 + \cos(\pi \cdot t/T)\bigr)$. Starts at $\eta_{\max}$, ends at $\eta_{\min}$, with the decay rate proportional to the slope of a cosine.

**Warmup + cosine:** for the first $W$ steps, $\eta(t) = \eta_{\max} \cdot (t / W)$ (linear ramp from 0 to $\eta_{\max}$). For $t \geq W$, $\eta(t) = \eta_{\min} + (\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min}) \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(1 + \cos(\pi \cdot (t - W) / (T - W))\bigr)$. The conventional modern default.

    if t < warmup:
        return peak * (t / warmup)            # linear ramp 0 → peak
    if t >= total:
        return floor                          # training is over; pin at floor
    # Cosine decay peak → floor over the remaining (total − warmup) steps.
    remaining = total - warmup
    return floor + (peak - floor) * 0.5 * (
        1 + math.cos(math.pi * (t - warmup) / remaining)
    )`}

<Callout title="Why warmup helps Adam" targetInteractive="schedule-explorer">
  Adam's per-parameter step size depends on its running statistics $m$ and $v$. At step 0, $m = v = 0$ for every parameter, and the bias-corrected values are tiny — Adam's first few steps are intentionally small. With a constant peak LR, those first few steps have a large *global* multiplier, which the bias correction can't offset. Warmup gives Adam a few steps to build up its statistics before the LR is large.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

**The LR schedule has the same shape, almost regardless of the problem.** Linear warmup over the first 1–5% of training, then cosine decay from peak to (often) 0 over the remaining 95–99%. The exact fraction of warmup and the exact peak LR are tuned, but the *shape* is conventional. The schedule is the cheapest way to get a free improvement on most training runs.

**The peak LR and the schedule are coupled, not independent.** A peak LR that's too high will diverge the model regardless of the schedule. The schedule controls how the LR *changes*; it can't rescue a peak that's wrong. The right way to pick the peak LR is to run a short training with a constant LR and find the largest one that doesn't diverge, then pick a peak 3–10× smaller. Once the peak is right, the schedule is decoration.

**Cosine decay is a default, not a law.** Other schedules work: linear decay, step decay, exponential decay. Cosine is conventional because its shape (fast decay near the end, slow decay early) matches the empirical shape of training loss curves — most of the loss reduction happens in the first half of training, and the late steps are fine-grained settling. The differences between these schedules are usually small; the difference between *a* schedule and *no* schedule (constant) is large.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **A learning-rate schedule is the LR as a function of training step**, and the conventional modern default is warmup + cosine decay. **Warmup fixes the early instability** (especially for Adam), and **decay fixes the late oscillation**. **The peak LR is the only thing that needs careful tuning**; the schedule's *shape* is conventional and the differences between schedules are small. The optimizer is fixed, the data is sampled, the model is fixed — the only remaining knob is the LR schedule.
