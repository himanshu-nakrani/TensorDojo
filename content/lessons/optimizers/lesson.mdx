## The SGD step is one equation: θ ← θ − η · g. Are there better equations?

The previous two lessons wrote a single optimizer step: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \cdot g$. With a careful learning rate, that step makes progress on toy problems. On real loss surfaces, the step has two failure modes the toy didn't expose:

1. **Narrow valleys.** A loss surface shaped like a long narrow valley (steep in one direction, shallow in the perpendicular direction) makes SGD oscillate across the valley walls. Each step is a bit too large in the steep direction, so the next step has to compensate in the opposite direction, and progress down the valley is slow.

2. **Plateaus and ravines.** Long plateaus of low gradient followed by sharp drops make SGD crawl through the plateau (the gradient is small, the steps are small) and then over-shoot the drop (the gradient suddenly gets large).

**Momentum** carries a running velocity (an exponential moving average of past gradients) and steps along the velocity, not the gradient. This smooths the path through noisy gradients and accelerates through plateaus. **Adam** adds a per-parameter adaptive step size: parameters that consistently receive large gradients get smaller steps; parameters that consistently receive small gradients get larger steps. Most modern training uses Adam by default.

## The formulas

**SGD (recap, for contrast):**
$$
\theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \eta \cdot g_t
$$

**SGD with momentum** (Polyak, "heavy ball"):
$$
v_{t+1} \;=\; \beta \cdot v_t + g_t, \qquad \theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \eta \cdot v_{t+1}
$$

The velocity $v$ is the exponential moving average of past gradients. $\beta = 0.9$ is the conventional default — $v$ keeps roughly the last 10 gradients' worth of history. The step direction is $v$, not the current gradient; this smooths out per-step noise and lets the optimizer maintain direction through plateaus.

**Adam** (Kingma & Ba, 2014):
$$
m_{t+1} \;=\; \beta_1 \cdot m_t + (1 - \beta_1) \cdot g_t \\
v_{t+1} \;=\; \beta_2 \cdot v_t + (1 - \beta_2) \cdot g_t^2 \\
\hat{m}_{t+1} \;=\; m_{t+1} / (1 - \beta_1^{t+1}) \\
\hat{v}_{t+1} \;=\; v_{t+1} / (1 - \beta_2^{t+1}) \\
\theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \eta \cdot \hat{m}_{t+1} / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_{t+1}} + \epsilon)
$$

The two running averages $m$ and $v$ estimate the first and second moments of the gradient. The bias-correction $\hat{m}, \hat{v}$ matters at the start of training when $m$ and $v$ are still small (initialized at zero); without it, the first few steps would be biased small. The step is the bias-corrected mean divided by the bias-corrected standard deviation — approximately $\eta \cdot \text{sign}(g)$ per parameter.

  code={`# Adam, the conventional defaults.

def adam_step(theta, g, m, v, t, eta=1e-3, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.999, eps=1e-8):
    m = beta1 * m + (1 - beta1) * g                # first moment
    v = beta2 * v + (1 - beta2) * g * g            # second moment
    m_hat = m / (1 - beta1 ** (t + 1))             # bias correction
    v_hat = v / (1 - beta2 ** (t + 1))
    theta = theta - eta * m_hat / (v_hat ** 0.5 + eps)
    return theta, m, v`}
  caption="Adam: maintain per-parameter running first and second moments of the gradient. The step is the bias-corrected mean divided by the bias-corrected standard deviation."

## Reading the result

**Momentum is a low-pass filter on the gradient signal.** The per-step gradient is noisy (especially at small batch sizes); the running velocity averages it out. On smooth loss surfaces the velocity is essentially the gradient; on noisy or oscillating ones, the velocity reveals the underlying direction the per-step gradient is hiding.

**Adam is approximately per-parameter sign descent with a global step size.** The ratio $\hat{m} / (\sqrt{\hat{v}} + \epsilon)$ is close to $\text{sign}(g)$ — the *direction* of the gradient, not the magnitude. The global LR scales that direction. This is why Adam is robust to gradient scale: a parameter that always gets gradients of magnitude 1000 has the same step size as a parameter that always gets gradients of magnitude 0.001, after a few warmup steps when $m$ and $v$ have caught up. Plain SGD would treat the first parameter as a thousand times more important than the second.

**The conventional defaults are good defaults, not arbitrary ones.** $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.999$ correspond to "remember roughly the last 10 gradients" and "remember roughly the last 1000 gradients" — about 10× the first-moment window. You usually don't need to tune them; tune the LR first.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **SGD with momentum is plain SGD with a low-pass filter on the gradient**: the running velocity smooths per-step noise and reveals the underlying direction. **Adam is approximately per-parameter sign descent**: the step is $\eta \cdot \text{sign}(g)$ for each parameter, after bias correction, which makes it robust to gradient scale. **Most modern training uses Adam by default**; the conventional $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$ work well across most problems — tune the LR first.
