## Fine-tuning a 70B model on the GPU that runs Cyberpunk

The LoRA lesson showed how to fine-tune a large model by freezing its weights and training only a tiny low-rank update — 0.1% of the parameters. That trick collapses the **trainable** parameter count, but the *base* model still has to live in memory for the forward and backward pass. A 70B model at 16-bit precision is 140 GB of weights, plus activations, plus gradients — easily 280 GB total. A single H100 is 80 GB. So even with LoRA, you need 4 GPUs to fine-tune a 70B model in 16-bit.

**QLoRA**, introduced in a 2023 paper by Dettmers et al., closes that last gap. The idea is to stack two tricks:

1. **Quantize the frozen base to 4 bits.** The 70B model now takes 35 GB instead of 140 GB — small enough to fit on one consumer GPU.
2. **Keep the LoRA adapters in 16-bit, train as normal.** The adapters are tiny (often under 1% of the base), so 16-bit precision for them costs almost nothing.

The result: a 70B model fine-tuned on a single 24 GB consumer GPU. The 2023 paper trained Guanaco-65B on one GPU in 24 hours and matched ChatGPT on the Vicuna benchmark. Every modern "fine-tune Llama-3-70B at home" recipe is QLoRA underneath.

## The formula

The forward pass for a linear layer in QLoRA is:

$$
\mathbf{y} = \operatorname{dequantize}(\mathbf{W}_{\text{4bit}}) \cdot \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}
$$

where $\mathbf{W}_{\text{4bit}}$ is the frozen base weight stored in 4 bits, and $\mathbf{B}\mathbf{A}$ is the LoRA adapter (rank $r$ matrices, kept in 16 bits, trained). The 4-bit base is **dequantized on the fly** to 16 bits during the forward pass; the dequantization is cheap (lookup table) and adds negligible latency.

The training signal flows only through the adapter:

- No gradients on $\mathbf{W}_{\text{4bit}}$ (frozen).
- Gradients on $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ via standard backprop.
- Optimizer state only for $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ — AdamW's $m$ and $v$ moments dominate memory in full fine-tuning, and QLoRA reduces them by the LoRA factor.

The 2023 paper added two technical refinements that matter in practice:

- **NF4** (NormalFloat 4-bit): a 4-bit quantization scheme optimized for normally-distributed weights. Lower error than uniform 4-bit.
- **Double quantization**: even the quantization constants are quantized, saving another ~0.4 bits per parameter on average.
- **Paged optimizer states**: optimizer memory spills to CPU when GPU memory spikes (e.g., long sequences), then pages back when needed.

<Callout title="Why 4-bit doesn't hurt quality" targetInteractive="qlora-memory">
  Two things. First, the LoRA adapter is in 16-bit — so any small reconstruction error in the 4-bit base can be absorbed by the trainable adapter during fine-tuning. Second, NF4 is calibrated to the empirical distribution of LLM weights (roughly normal, centered at zero, narrow), so the quantization error is much smaller than naive 4-bit. The 2023 paper showed QLoRA-trained models matched 16-bit LoRA within a few tenths of a benchmark point on MMLU and Vicuna.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

**The base model is frozen and quantized.** No gradients touch it. The 4-bit representation is read-only at training time; weights are dequantized on-the-fly to 16-bit during the matmul, then discarded. The 4-bit storage is the only persistent copy.

**LoRA adapters carry the entire training signal.** They are kept in 16-bit (or sometimes bf16), and the optimizer state (Adam $m$, $v$) is computed only for the adapter parameters. This is the same LoRA construction as the previous lesson — the only change is what's *underneath* the adapter.

**Memory savings come from three sources, multiplied.** (1) 4-bit base saves 4× on weight storage. (2) LoRA collapses gradient and optimizer storage to the adapter's size, a 100× reduction in the trainable count. (3) Gradient checkpointing (often paired with QLoRA) trades extra forward FLOPs for lower activation memory.

**Quality is basically unchanged.** The original paper benchmarked QLoRA fine-tunes on MMLU, Vicuna, and BBH against 16-bit LoRA and against fully fine-tuned models, and found differences in the noise. Subsequent work has confirmed: for adapter-based fine-tuning, 4-bit base is essentially free.

## A second look

QLoRA is a stack of three independent tricks: NF4 quantization, LoRA, and paged optimizers. None of them is novel in isolation — 4-bit quantization existed, LoRA existed, optimizer state offloading existed. The contribution was showing they could be combined without quality loss, with a calibrated NF4 scheme that respected the empirical weight distribution. The 2023 paper is a great example of "the whole stack matters more than the individual tricks."

<Callout title="What about inference?" targetInteractive="qlora-memory">
  QLoRA is a *training* technique; inference uses the quantized base with the LoRA adapter merged in. After training, you can either keep the adapter as a separate small module (useful for serving multiple fine-tunes from one base) or fold it into the base weights for deployment. Both are common in production — adapter-per-task is the trick that lets a single foundation model serve hundreds of fine-tuned variants without 100× the storage.
</Callout>

## Recap

Three takeaways. **QLoRA = 4-bit frozen base + 16-bit LoRA adapter.** The base provides capability; the adapter carries the fine-tune. **Memory drops 10–25× vs full fine-tuning** with negligible quality loss — the trainable part is small enough that optimizer state and gradients are cheap, and the base is small enough that it fits on one GPU. **It democratized LLM fine-tuning.** Before QLoRA, fine-tuning a 70B model needed a server. After, it ran on a single H100 or two consumer GPUs. The recipe is now standard across HuggingFace's PEFT library, axolotl, Unsloth, and every "fine-tune at home" tutorial you'll find. This wraps up the fine-tuning track — and the curriculum has now covered the model end to end: from the dot product at lesson 1 to the practical recipe that fine-tunes a frontier model in a weekend.
