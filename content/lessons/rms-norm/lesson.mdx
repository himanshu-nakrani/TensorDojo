## Same job, fewer ops

The previous lesson introduced **LayerNorm**: re-center each token's vector to zero mean, divide by its standard deviation, then apply a learned scale $\gamma$ and shift $\beta$. That keeps activations on a stable scale across depth, which is why a 24-layer transformer can train.

But every term in that formula costs something. **Subtracting the mean** costs a reduction across the feature dimension. **Adding the learned shift $\beta$** costs an extra parameter vector per layer. And on long-context, large-$d_{\text{model}}$ models, those two extras add up — both in FLOPs and in memory bandwidth on every forward and backward pass.

In 2019 Zhang and Sennrich asked the obvious question: *do we actually need them?* They tried dropping the mean step (and the shift), keeping only the variance step (with no mean subtraction) and the learned scale. Empirically: equivalent quality, ~10–30% faster norm, no instability. The simplified version is **RMSNorm**, and it's what Llama, Mistral, Gemma, Qwen, and most modern LLMs use today.

## The formula

LayerNorm normalizes by the standard deviation — which requires first computing a mean. RMSNorm normalizes by the **root mean square** instead, which is the same formula *without* the mean subtraction:

$$
\operatorname{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \gamma \cdot \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\operatorname{RMS}(\mathbf{x})}, \qquad
\operatorname{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i^2 + \epsilon}
$$

  code={`import numpy as np

def layer_norm(x, gamma, beta, eps=1e-5):
    mu = x.mean(axis=-1, keepdims=True)              # one extra reduction
    var = x.var(axis=-1, keepdims=True)
    x_hat = (x - mu) / np.sqrt(var + eps)
    return gamma * x_hat + beta                       # one extra parameter

def rms_norm(x, gamma, eps=1e-5):
    rms = np.sqrt((x ** 2).mean(axis=-1, keepdims=True) + eps)
    return gamma * (x / rms)                          # no mean, no beta`}
  caption="Two reductions become one. The bias parameter beta is also gone — modern transformers run with no bias on attention/MLP linears either, for the same reason: cheaper, no quality hit."

That's the empirical reason RMSNorm "just works" — the mean it would have subtracted was already close to zero anyway.

## Reading the result

**Mathematically: RMSNorm = LayerNorm with the mean term deleted.** That is the entire change. The learned shift $\beta$ goes with it (with no mean to absorb, there's nothing for $\beta$ to compensate against, and removing it has no measurable effect). Everything else is identical: per-token, per-position normalization, learned scale, $\epsilon$ for numerical safety.

**Empirically: same quality, faster.** The 2019 paper showed equivalent perplexity on translation and language modeling. Every subsequent large-model paper that compared the two has found the same: no quality loss, ~10–30% wall-clock speedup on the norm op. The norm is a small fraction of total runtime, but on a model that runs for months, every percent counts.

**Why it caught on.** Llama (2023) shipped with RMSNorm and pre-norm placement (norm before the sublayer, not after). Every modern open-weights LLM family — Llama, Mistral, Gemma, Qwen, DeepSeek — uses the same recipe. The "norm" in a 2025 transformer almost certainly means RMSNorm; "LayerNorm" is still common in older or smaller models, and in production code you'll see both.

## A second look

The geometric difference is small but real. LayerNorm projects an input vector onto the hyperplane through the origin perpendicular to the all-ones direction (subtracting the mean), then renormalizes. RMSNorm just renormalizes. In high dimensions and zero-mean residual streams, these produce nearly identical outputs.

<Callout title="Why this works at all" targetInteractive="rms-norm-compare">
  The intuition: a norm's job is to keep the magnitude of the residual stream bounded across depth. Re-centering the mean was a secondary effect — useful for numerical conditioning but not load-bearing. Once people noticed, dropping it was free.
</Callout>

## Recap

Three takeaways. **RMSNorm = LayerNorm without the mean.** Divide by root-mean-square instead of standard deviation, keep the learned scale $\gamma$, drop the learned shift $\beta$. **Same quality, fewer ops.** Empirically equivalent perplexity, ~10–30% faster norm wall-clock. **It's the modern default.** Llama, Mistral, Gemma, Qwen — every major recent LLM family uses it. When you see "norm" in a transformer paper from 2023 onward, assume RMSNorm unless told otherwise.
