## How does a probability distribution become a single output token?

The previous lesson closed the architecture: stack $N$ transformer blocks, embed at the bottom, project to vocabulary at the top, and you have a model. The output of the projection is a vector of **logits** — one real number per vocabulary entry. To *generate* a single token, you need a rule that turns the logits into a single integer.

The four most common rules are **greedy decoding**, **temperature sampling**, **top-k sampling**, and **top-p (nucleus) sampling**. They differ in how much of the distribution they keep, and how much of the model's uncertainty they let through.

## The formula

Given the model's output logits $\mathbf{z} = (z_0, z_1, \ldots, z_{V-1})$:

- **Greedy**: $\text{out} = \arg\max_i z_i$. Pick the highest logit. Deterministic.
- **Temperature sampling**: $\mathbf{p} = \operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z}/T)$, then sample one index. $T = 1$ is the canonical softmax; $T < 1$ sharpens, $T > 1$ flattens.
- **Top-k**: keep the $k$ highest logits, zero the rest, renormalize, sample. $k = 1$ collapses to greedy; $k = V$ is plain temperature sampling.
- **Top-p (nucleus)**: keep the smallest set of entries whose cumulative probability $\geq p$, renormalize, sample. Adaptive: when the distribution is peaked, the nucleus is small; when it is flat, the nucleus is large.

  code={`import numpy as np

def softmax(x, T=1.0):
    z = (x - x.max()) / T
    e = np.exp(z)
    return e / e.sum()

def greedy(logits):
    return int(np.argmax(logits))

def temperature_sample(logits, T=1.0, rng=np.random):
    probs = softmax(logits, T)
    return rng.choice(len(probs), p=probs)

def topk_sample(logits, k=5, T=1.0, rng=np.random):
    probs = softmax(logits, T)
    # Keep the k highest-prob entries. Ties at the boundary are kept,
    # so the support can be slightly larger than k in pathological cases.
    cutoff = np.sort(probs)[-k]
    kept = np.where(probs >= cutoff, probs, 0)
    kept /= kept.sum()
    return rng.choice(len(kept), p=kept)

def topp_sample(logits, p=0.9, T=1.0, rng=np.random):
    probs = softmax(logits, T)
    sorted_idx = np.argsort(-probs)
    cumsum = np.cumsum(probs[sorted_idx])
    n_keep = int(np.searchsorted(cumsum, p)) + 1
    kept = np.zeros_like(probs)
    kept[sorted_idx[:n_keep]] = probs[sorted_idx[:n_keep]]
    kept /= kept.sum()
    return rng.choice(len(kept), p=kept)`}

## Reading the result

**The strategy is *separate* from the model.** The model produces logits; the strategy decides how to interpret them. The same logits can produce wildly different output streams depending on whether you greedy-decode, sample with low temperature, or use a top-p nucleus.

**Temperature trades off coherence and variety.** $T \to 0$ makes the model deterministic (always picks the top logit). $T \to \infty$ makes it uniform (samples from the full vocabulary). Most real systems use a temperature in between, often $T \approx 0.7$ to $1.0$, with the exact value tuned for the use case.

**Top-k and top-p are ways to *bound* the sampling.** Even at $T = 1$, sampling from a 50,000-token vocabulary can produce rare and surprising tokens. Top-k and top-p cut off the long tail: top-k by absolute count, top-p by cumulative mass.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Decoding is the rule that turns logits into a single output token.** Greedy picks the top logit (deterministic). Temperature samples from a softened distribution ($T < 1$ sharpens, $T > 1$ flattens — the same temperature from the softmax lesson). Top-k and top-p bound the sampling to the most likely entries. **The model and the strategy are independent.** The same model can be sampled with any strategy; the choice depends on the use case. **In production, temperature is almost always between 0 and 1, with top-p or top-k applied** to bound the long tail. That number — the loss for one prediction — is cross-entropy.
