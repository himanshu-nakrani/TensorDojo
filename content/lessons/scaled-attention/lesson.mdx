## Motivation

The attention lesson used $\mathbf{Q} \cdot \mathbf{K}$ as the score. That score is a sum of $d_k$ products of $N(0, 1)$ random variables. The variance of one such product is $1$, and the variance of the sum is $d_k$. So as $d_k$ grows — from $16$ in early transformers to $128$ in modern heads — the raw score values spread wider. Wide scores push $\operatorname{softmax}$ to one entry; a one-hot softmax has zero gradient; the model stops learning.

The fix is a single number: $\sqrt{d_k}$. Divide every score by $\sqrt{d_k}$ and the variance returns to $1$ regardless of dimension. Pre-softmax scores look the same at any $d_k$. Softmax no longer saturates by default. This is the "scaled dot-product attention" that the original Transformer paper introduced, and it is the single most important numerical trick in the architecture.

## The formula

For a $d_k$-dimensional embedding dimension:

$$
\operatorname{score}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{d_k}}
$$

## Reading the result

Two things to internalize.

1. **Variance grows linearly with $d_k$.** $d_k = 1$ is a thin sliver; $d_k = 16$ is four times as wide; $d_k = 64$ is eight times as wide. Each is a Gaussian with mean zero and standard deviation $\sqrt{d_k}$. The numbers above the chart confirm this — they read the empirical standard deviation of the $4{,}000$ samples and compare it to the theoretical $\sqrt{d_k}$.
2. **The scale toggle brings every $d_k$ back to a unit bell.** Toggle on at $d_k = 128$ and the histogram is identical in shape to the one at $d_k = 1$. The toggle does not change the *shape* of the data — only the x-axis scale. The variance is $1$ at every dimension.

This is the same reason the original Transformer paper introduced the factor: with $d_k = 64$ and no scaling, the dot product has standard deviation $8$, the largest scores are around $\pm 24$, and $\operatorname{softmax}$ of those scores is effectively a step function. With scaling, the scores are $\pm 3$ in the typical case, and $\operatorname{softmax}$ is in its linear regime.

## Three experiments

**1. Saturation in action.** Set $d_k = 64$ and the toggle off. The histogram is so wide that the right tail reaches $+5$ or beyond. Now $\operatorname{softmax}$ of the row of scores in a real attention head, where the *largest* score might be $+12$ and the rest are $0$ to $+2$, puts $\approx 1.0$ mass on the largest entry. The gradient of softmax with respect to the input is $\approx 0$ there. No learning.

**2. Why $\sqrt{d_k}$ specifically.** If we divided by $d_k$, the scores would shrink too fast: the standard deviation would be $1/\sqrt{d_k} \to 0$ as $d_k$ grew, the softmax would approach a uniform distribution, and the model would not learn to attend. $\sqrt{d_k}$ is the unique divisor that normalises the variance to a constant, independent of $d_k$. The proof is the variance calculation: $\operatorname{Var}(\sum_i q_i k_i) = \sum_i \operatorname{Var}(q_i)\operatorname{Var}(k_i) = d_k$ for independent unit Gaussians, and the standard deviation is $\sqrt{d_k}$.

**3. Independence of dimension.** Toggle the scale on and step through $d_k = 1, 8, 64, 128$. The histogram shape does not change. The x-axis labels change (it shows the *scaled* value), but the bell-curve shape is constant. This is the whole point: the architecture can use whatever dimension it wants, and the downstream softmax sees the same distribution of scores every time.

## A second look

The same four-token attention matrix from the previous lesson, here as a sanity check: with the score matrix computed the *unscaled* way, the weight matrix in a real head would saturate; with the score matrix computed the scaled way, the weights stay in the linear regime of softmax.

<Callout targetInteractive="attention-matrix-scaled">

The other weights in the same row are $\approx 0$. Now imagine if every score in the row were divided by $\sqrt{d_k}$ first: the values would shrink back to the linear regime and the weight distribution would be informative.
</Callout>

The attention matrix interactive here does not divide by $\sqrt{d_k}$ internally (it is the same component as the previous lesson). The point is to put a concrete number on "what unscaled attention looks like" — a $4.0$ score becomes a $\approx 1.0$ weight and a $-1.0$ score becomes a $\approx 0$ weight, and the gradient with respect to the score is $\approx 0$ at both ends.

## Recap

For unit-Gaussian $Q$ and $K$ vectors of dimension $d_k$, the dot product has variance $d_k$. Wide scores mean $\operatorname{softmax}$ saturates and gradients vanish. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the variance to $1$ at every dimension. This is the single line of code that makes scaled dot-product attention trainable at modern widths. The trick is purely a matter of *units*: it does not change what the model attends to, only the numerical scale of the scores that go into softmax.
