## You have $10M of compute. How many parameters?

The training loop you built two lessons ago could in principle scale to any model size. What it can't tell you is *which* size you should pick. Before any kernel runs, before any data loads, the very first question is: given a fixed compute budget (which is what a real GPU cluster amounts to), how big should the model be, and how many tokens should you train it on?

For most of the 2010s the answer was "make it bigger." Add parameters; loss falls. GPT-2 was 1.5B parameters trained on ~10B tokens. GPT-3 was 175B parameters trained on 300B tokens. Gopher was 280B trained on 300B. Each step jumped the parameter count by an order of magnitude; each step saw lower loss. The field assumed parameters were what mattered.

Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) showed that was wrong. At GPT-3's compute budget, you could have trained a much smaller model on far more tokens and ended up with lower loss. GPT-3 was *under-trained* on its parameter count. Gopher was *worse*-under-trained. The whole pre-2022 frontier was sitting on the wrong half of the trade.

## The functional form

Kaplan et al. (2020) first showed that LM loss decays as a power law in parameter count $N$, token count $D$, and compute $C$ — separately. Chinchilla put the three together into a single fitted surface:

$$
L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}
$$

Three terms: an irreducible floor $E$ (the entropy of the data itself), a parameter-deficit term that shrinks as $N$ grows, and a data-deficit term that shrinks as $D$ grows. The fitted constants (Approach 3 from the paper) are $E = 1.69$, $A = 406.4$, $B = 410.7$, $\alpha = 0.34$, $\beta = 0.28$.

The compute constraint comes from the standard FLOPs estimate: training a dense transformer takes $C \approx 6 N D$ FLOPs (the 6 is 2 from multiply-accumulate × 3 from forward + backward). Fix $C$, and you're tracing a curve through (N, D) space; minimize loss along that curve and you've found the compute-optimal split.

  code={`# Sweep along the C = 6 · N · D constraint and find the optimum.
def chinchilla_optimum(C, log_n_grid=200):
    best = (None, float('inf'))
    for log_n in np.linspace(6, 13, log_n_grid):
        N = 10**log_n
        D = C / (6 * N)
        L = 1.69 + 406.4 * N**-0.34 + 410.7 * D**-0.28
        if L < best[1]:
            best = (N, L)
    N_star = best[0]
    return N_star, C / (6 * N_star)

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

**Loss is a smooth, fitted function of N and D.** The Chinchilla functional form $L = E + A/N^\alpha + B/D^\beta$ is an empirical fit, not a theorem. It generalizes well across the 2020-2022 scale range; whether it extrapolates to today's frontier scale is contested. But within its fitting range, it makes a specific testable prediction: given $C$, there is a unique compute-optimal $N$, and being off it costs measurable loss.

**Pre-Chinchilla, the field was systematically wrong in one direction.** GPT-3, Gopher, MT-NLG — all parameter-rich, all under-trained. The intuition that "more parameters = better" wasn't wrong, but the implicit corollary "even at fixed compute" was. Chinchilla showed the trade-off was steeper than people thought, and that smaller-and-longer beat bigger-and-shorter at any reasonable compute budget.

**Post-Chinchilla, production over-trains on purpose.** The Chinchilla optimum minimizes training compute. Real systems minimize total cost = training + inference × queries. For any model that will serve millions of requests, the inference cost dominates, and the right move is to over-train a smaller model. LLaMA-3 (70B, 15T tokens, ~214 tokens per parameter) is the clearest example: ~10× past Chinchilla-optimal, deliberately, because the served-token-count justifies it.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Loss decays as a power law in N and D**; the Chinchilla functional form is $L = E + A/N^\alpha + B/D^\beta$ with fitted constants from real LM data. **For a fixed compute budget $C \approx 6 N D$, there is a unique compute-optimal split** — pre-Chinchilla models systematically sat on the param-rich, token-poor side of it. **Production over-trains on purpose** because inference cost is its own budget, and a smaller, more-trained model is cheaper to serve. The 20-tokens-per-parameter headline rule from Chinchilla is roughly right at training budgets near theirs; modern production runs at 50–200 tokens per parameter.

Even a Chinchilla-optimal model eventually overfits if you train it long enough on the same tokens. The next lesson backs up from "how big should the model be" to "even with the right size, why does test loss eventually climb?" — the overfitting story the regularization chapter opens with.
