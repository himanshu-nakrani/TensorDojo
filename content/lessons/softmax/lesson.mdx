## How do three scores become a choice?

A language model has three candidate next tokens with raw scores $+3.2$, $+0.7$, $-1.4$. To *pick* one, you need a probability distribution — non-negative weights that sum to one. **Softmax** is the function that turns raw reals into that distribution, and the temperature dial is the only knob that controls how peaked the output is.

The interactive below shows five scores and the resulting distribution. Drag the temperature; watch what happens to the bars.

## The formula

$$
\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{x})_i \;=\; \frac{e^{x_i}}{\sum_j e^{x_j}}
$$

Each score is exponentiated (always positive), then divided by the sum of all the exponentiated scores. The result is non-negative and sums to one.

<MathCode
  math="\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{x}, T)_i = \frac{e^{x_i / T}}{\sum_j e^{x_j / T}}"
  code={`def softmax(x, T=1.0):
    z = (x - x.max()) / T   # subtract the max for stability
    e = np.exp(z)
    return e / e.sum()`}
  caption="Numerically stable softmax in NumPy — subtract the max so the largest exponent is 0."
/>

<Callout title="Why subtract the max" targetInteractive="softmax-explorer">
  If the scores are $[1000, 1001, 1002]$, naive $\exp$ overflows. Subtracting the max gives $[0, 1, 2]$ — perfectly tame — and the ratios are identical because dividing numerator and denominator by the same constant leaves the quotient unchanged.
</Callout>

<Callout title="What to watch for" targetInteractive="softmax-explorer">
  Set the first score to $0.1$ and the second to $2.0$. At $T = 0.1$ the second bar is essentially $1.0$ and the rest are pinned at zero — the gap of $1.9$ has been blown up by the exponential. Slide $T$ up to $3.0$ and the second bar drops to ${\sim}0.27$ while the others rise toward an even share. The *winner* never changes — softmax preserves argmax for any $T > 0$ — but the *contrast* slides smoothly from peaked to flat. That smooth contrast control is what makes temperature useful.
</Callout>

## Reading the result

Two facts to internalize.

**Low $T$ sharpens.** A small $T$ divides every score by a small number, so the exponential amplifies their differences. The largest score wins almost all the mass; the rest are pushed toward zero. The model commits to its top guess.

**High $T$ flattens.** A large $T$ shrinks the differences, and softmax approaches a uniform distribution. The model explores.

$T = 1$ is the canonical definition; every other temperature is a deliberate choice about how peaked you want the distribution to be.

The exponential does most of the work. It is positive everywhere, which is why the output is non-negative, and it amplifies differences between the inputs. A difference of $1$ in score space becomes a ratio of $e$ in output space; a difference of $4$ becomes a ratio of $e^4 \approx 55$. Small changes in the input become large changes in the output. That is also why softmax is brittle at low temperature: a single large score absorbs almost all the mass, and any small perturbation to the runner-up has zero visible effect on the distribution. The temperature dial is what walks the output smoothly between these two regimes — committed and exploratory.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **Softmax turns arbitrary reals into a non-negative distribution that sums to one.** Every exponentiated score is divided by the sum of all exponentiated scores, so the result is always a valid probability. **Temperature $T$ controls contrast:** low $T$ peaks on the largest input, high $T$ spreads mass roughly evenly. **Subtracting the maximum before exponentiating is what makes the function numerically safe** — the ratios are unchanged because dividing numerator and denominator by the same constant leaves the quotient intact.
