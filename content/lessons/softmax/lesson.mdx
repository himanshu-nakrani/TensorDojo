## Motivation

Models emit raw numbers. A classifier might score three candidate tokens at $+3.2$, $+0.7$, and $-1.4$. A reinforcement-learning policy might value three moves at $1.0$, $0.8$, and $0.95$. None of these numbers carry meaning on their own — they are just real-valued signals of relative preference.

The next step almost always requires a *distribution*: a set of non-negative weights that sum to one. We need one to pick a token, to sample a move, to weight components of a mixture, to interpret an attention head. The conversion from raw scores to a probability distribution is the job of the **softmax function**, and it is small enough to hold in your head and wide enough to show up almost everywhere in deep learning.

The only knob the user controls is **temperature**. Master that knob and you have the contrast control behind sampling, decoding, and attention.

## The formula

$$
\operatorname{softmax}(x_i) \;=\; \frac{e^{x_i}}{\sum_j e^{x_j}}
$$

Each score is exponentiated (which is always positive) and then divided by the sum of the exponentiated scores. The result is non-negative, sums to one, and assigns more mass to larger inputs.

The implementation has a subtlety that is the entire reason the function is *implementable* in floating point at all.

<MathCode
  math="\operatorname{softmax}(x_i, T) = \frac{e^{\,x_i / T}}{\sum_j e^{\,x_j / T}}"
  code={`def softmax(x, T=1.0):
    z = (x - x.max()) / T
    e = np.exp(z)
    return e / e.sum()`}
  caption="Numerically stable softmax in NumPy"
/>

The `x - x.max()` step is not optional. If your scores are $[1000,\ 1001,\ 1002]$, the naive $e^{x_i}$ overflows. Subtracting the max gives $[0,\ 1,\ 2]$ — perfectly tame — and the ratios come out identical because dividing numerator and denominator by the same constant leaves the quotient unchanged.

## Reading the result

Two facts to internalize.

**Low temperature sharpens.** A small $T$ divides every score by a small number, so differences between scores are *amplified* by the exponential. The largest score wins almost all the mass and the rest are pushed toward zero. This is the regime used at decoding time when you want the model to commit to its top guess.

**High temperature flattens.** A large $T$ shrinks the differences, and the softmax approaches a uniform distribution over the entries. This is the regime used when you want the model to explore — sample a less-likely token, mix among several reasonable moves, encourage diverse outputs.

The same function, the same formula, the only dial is how much contrast you want. $T = 1$ is the canonical definition; every other temperature is a deliberate choice about how peaked you want the distribution to be.

## Three experiments

**1. The amplification effect.** Set $T$ to $0.2$. The largest score wins nearly all the mass. Now increase just one of the smaller scores by $1.0$ — say, raise the fourth input from $-0.5$ to $0.5$. Watch the entire distribution reshuffle: a small change in inputs, amplified by the exponential, causes a large change in outputs. This is the same math that makes a sharp distribution brittle.

**2. The slow start, fast finish.** Set $T$ to $3.0$. The five probabilities are within a few hundredths of each other. Now drag the slider slowly toward $0.1$. The bars barely move for the first half of the drag, then collapse to one almost-all-the-mass winner in the last fraction. The exponential compresses: most of the action happens at the bottom of the temperature range.

**3. The constant case.** Set all five scores to the same value, say $1.0$. No matter where you drag the temperature, the distribution stays exactly uniform. $T$ is a *contrast* knob, not a *shift* knob — it cannot invent preference that is not in the input.

Notice how the handoff is *not* smooth: the second-place bar does not just creep up. At some score value, the exponential weighting swings the winner in a single step.

That is the same behaviour the temperature slider controls — the temperature is just a smooth way to walk through these handoffs, with the threshold of each handoff determined by the relative sizes of the scores.

## Recap

Softmax turns arbitrary reals into a probability distribution. The temperature $T$ controls the contrast: low $T$ peaks on the largest input, high $T$ spreads mass roughly evenly. Subtracting the maximum before exponentiating is not a cosmetic improvement — it is what makes the function safe to call on real model outputs. The next time you see a model's decoding temperature, a sampling distribution in RL, or an attention softmax, this is the function doing the work.
