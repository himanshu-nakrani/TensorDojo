## How does a model represent the word "cat"?

A model never sees the word "cat" as a string. It sees an integer id — say, 4231 — that points into a table. The table is a single matrix $E \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ with one row per token in the vocabulary. The row at index 4231 is the embedding of "cat": a $d$-dimensional vector that the model can do math with.

Before training, the rows are random. After training on enough text, the geometry becomes meaningful: similar words end up near each other, and the famous analogy $E[\text{king}] - E[\text{man}] + E[\text{woman}] \approx E[\text{queen}]$ emerges. The interactive below shows a hand-placed 2D slice of what an embedding plane *can* look like.

## The formula

$$
\mathbf{x}_i = E[i, :] \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
$$

One row lookup. No arithmetic, no transformation — just *what is*. The training process adjusts $E$ to make the dot products of related tokens large (so attention fires between them) and unrelated tokens small.

## Reading the result

Two facts to internalize.

**The embedding is the model's first decision about every word.** Everything downstream — attention, layer norm, the residual stream — sees only vectors, not words. The information about "this token is a verb" or "this token is plural" has to live in the geometry of those vectors. The 2D slice of an embedding space is a hint at that geometry, projected for the eye to read.

**Cosine similarity between two rows of $E$ is the model's first similarity judgment.** Attention scores, retrieval hits, nearest-neighbor lookups — all of these reduce to "is this row close to that row in $E$?". The interactive's find-nearest output is exactly what a $k$-NN retrieval system returns on top of an embedding model.

The takeaway: the embedding table is not a one-hot representation dressed up. It is a *learned* function from "token id" to "a point in a space the rest of the model can do math with." Training pushes the rows around until that space has the structure attention needs.

## Recap

A token embedding is a learnable lookup from id to vector. After training, the geometry of the rows is what carries every signal the model uses — similarity to other tokens, analogy structure, the meaning of words. The next lesson adds the other half of the input: not just *what* the token is, but *where* it sits in the sequence. Without that, attention cannot tell "the cat sat" apart from "sat cat the".
