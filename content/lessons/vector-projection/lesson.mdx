## Motivation

The dot product tells you *how aligned* two vectors are. Often the question is more specific: of the full vector **a**, how much of it actually lies along **b**? That is the *projection* of **a** onto **b** — a single vector on the line spanned by **b**, with the rest of **a** left over as the *residual*.

Projections are everywhere. In regression, the prediction is the projection of the target onto the column space of the features. In attention, the weighted sum $\sum_j \text{weight}_j V_j$ is a projection in disguise. In compression, low-rank approximations are projections onto a smaller subspace. The next step beyond the dot product is to ask: where, exactly, does this much of **a** sit on the line of **b**?

## The formula

For non-zero vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$:

$$
\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}} \, \mathbf{b}, \qquad
\operatorname{res}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} \;=\; \mathbf{a} - \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}
$$

The scalar coefficient $\frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}}$ is the only place the two vectors meet. Once you have it, $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}$ is just that scalar times $\mathbf{b}$.

## Reading the result

Three things to internalize.

1. **The projection has magnitude $\lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert \cos\theta$.** It is the shadow **a** casts on the line of **b**. When **a** is perpendicular to **b**, the shadow is zero. When **a** is parallel to **b**, the shadow is the full **a**.
2. **The residual is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{b}$.** That is the whole point of the construction. The residual contains exactly the information in **a** that is *not* aligned with **b**.
3. **On unit vectors, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \cos\theta$.** Toggle the "show unit vectors" view and verify. With both arrows stretched to length 1, the dot product is purely a function of the angle. The component form ($\sum a_i b_i$) and the geometric form ($\|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$) coincide.

## Three experiments

**1. Magnitude does not matter for direction.** Set $\mathbf{a} = (1.4, 0.0)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (1.0, 0.0)$. Now scale $\mathbf{a}$ to $(2.8, 0.0)$. The projection also doubles. The *ratio* $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} / \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert$ — that is, $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert \cos\theta$ divided by $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert$ — does not change. Cosine similarity is what survives.

**2. The residual is perpendicular, always.** Pick any $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$. The dashed red arrow's tip-to-origin angle is exactly $90°$ from $\mathbf{b}$. If you tilt **b**, the residual arrow tilts with it to keep the perpendicularity. This is the visual proof of "$\mathbf{a} = \operatorname{proj} + \operatorname{res}$, with $\operatorname{proj} \parallel \mathbf{b}$ and $\operatorname{res} \perp \mathbf{b}$".

**3. Cosine is a unit test for alignment.** Set $\mathbf{a} = (1.5, 0.0)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (1.0, 0.0)$. The dot product is $1.5$ but the cosine is $1.0$. Now make $\mathbf{b}$ twice as long. The dot product doubles to $3.0$ and the cosine stays at $1.0$. Cosine similarity is what tells you two vectors point the same way *regardless of how long they are*. That is exactly what the attention head needs.

## Recap

The projection $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}$ extracts the part of **a** that lies along **b**. Its magnitude is $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\cos\theta$. The residual is what is left over, and it is always perpendicular to **b**. On unit vectors, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \cos\theta$ — a pure direction similarity, indifferent to magnitude. The dot product carries both pieces of information at once; cosine is the one that survives normalization. That distinction is what shows up in attention, where every row of $Q$ is dotted with every row of $K^\top$, and where dividing by the row's magnitude is what keeps one key from drowning the others.
