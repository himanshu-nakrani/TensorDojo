## How much of **a** lies along **b**?

The dot product answers "how aligned?". A more specific question: of the full vector $\mathbf{a}$, how much of it actually lies along $\mathbf{b}$? That is the **projection** of $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$ — a single vector on the line spanned by $\mathbf{b}$, with the rest of $\mathbf{a}$ left over as the **residual**.

Projections are everywhere. In regression, the prediction is the projection of the target onto the column space of the features. In attention, the weighted sum $\sum_j w_j \mathbf{v}_j$ is a projection in disguise. The next step beyond the dot product is to ask: *where*, exactly, does this much of $\mathbf{a}$ sit on the line of $\mathbf{b}$?

## The formula

For non-zero vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$:

$$
\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}} \, \mathbf{b}, \qquad
\operatorname{res}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a} \;=\; \mathbf{a} - \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}
$$

The scalar coefficient $\frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}}$ is the only place the two vectors meet. Once you have it, $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}$ is just that scalar times $\mathbf{b}$.

## Reading the result

Three facts to internalize.

1. **The projection has magnitude $\|\mathbf{a}\| \cos\theta$.** It is the shadow $\mathbf{a}$ casts on the line of $\mathbf{b}$. When $\mathbf{a}$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{b}$, the shadow is zero. When $\mathbf{a}$ is parallel to $\mathbf{b}$, the shadow is the full $\mathbf{a}$.
2. **The residual is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{b}$.** $\mathbf{a} = \operatorname{proj} + \operatorname{res}$, with $\operatorname{proj} \parallel \mathbf{b}$ and $\operatorname{res} \perp \mathbf{b}$. The residual contains exactly the information in $\mathbf{a}$ that is *not* aligned with $\mathbf{b}$ — the part that is, by construction, orthogonal to $\mathbf{b}$.
3. **On unit vectors, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \cos\theta$.** Toggle the unit vectors and verify. The component form and the geometric form coincide.

A common confusion: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$ scales with both magnitudes; $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}$ scales with one. When you want a question that depends on *direction only*, divide by both magnitudes — that is **cosine similarity**.

Geometrically, projection is the closest point on the line spanned by $\mathbf{b}$ to the tip of $\mathbf{a}$. Every "fit $\mathbf{a}$ to a line through the origin" problem reduces to this. The residual is the distance from the tip to the line. The Pythagorean identity $\|\mathbf{a}\|^2 = \|\operatorname{proj}\|^2 + \|\operatorname{res}\|^2$ follows from the perpendicularity.

## Recap

Three takeaways. **The projection is a shadow on a line** — $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{b}} \mathbf{a}$ has magnitude $\|\mathbf{a}\| \cos\theta$ and lives on the line spanned by $\mathbf{b}$. **The residual is what is left**, and it is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{b}$: $\mathbf{a} = \operatorname{proj} + \operatorname{res}$, and $\operatorname{proj} \perp \operatorname{res}$. **On unit vectors, $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \cos\theta$ exactly** — the geometric and component forms coincide.
